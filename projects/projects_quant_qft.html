How can we build a circuit representation of the QFTQuantum Fourier Transform?

Represenation of input state vector in BCDBinary Coded Decimal format: \[ |x\rangle = |x_{1}x_{2}x_{3}\rangle \therefore x = \sum_{l=1}^{n}x_{l}2^{n-l} \]

The discrete quantum fourier transform is defined as follows: \[ |\tilde{x}\rangle = QFT|x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y=0}^{N-1}e^{\frac{2\pi{i}xy}{N}}|y\rangle \]

BCDBinary Coded Decimal expansion of the quantum fourier transform. \[ \begin{aligned} |\tilde{x}\rangle = QFT|x\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y=0}^{N-1} e^{\frac{2\pi{i}xy}{N}}|y\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y=0}^{N-1} e^{\frac{2\pi{i}x{\sum_{k=1}^{n}{\frac{y_{k}2^n}{2^k}}}}{N}}|y\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y=0}^{N-1} e^{2\pi{i}x{\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{y_k}{2^k}}}|y\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y=0}^{N-1} \prod_{k=1}^{n}e^{2\pi{i}x{\frac{y_k}{2^k}}}|y\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y_{1}=0}^{1}\sum_{y_{2}=0}^{1}\sum_{y_{3}=0}^{1}\ldots\sum_{y_{n}=0}^{1} \prod_{k=1}^{n}e^{2\pi{i}x{\frac{y_k}{2^k}}}|y_{1}y_{2}y_{3}\ldots y_{n}\rangle \end{aligned} \]

We can represent a number in base 2 expansion with a sum of powers: \[ \begin{aligned} \text{e.g: }y = 5_{10} &= 101_{2} \\ &= (1)2^{2} + (0)2^{1} + (1)2^{0} \\ &= \sum_{k = 1}^{3} \frac{y_{k}2^3}{2^k} \end{aligned} \]

Continuing with the expansion we now consider the number y as a tensor product of its BCD representation. \[ \begin{aligned} |\tilde{x}\rangle = QFT|x\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{y_{1}=0}^{1}\sum_{y_{2}=0}^{1}\sum_{y_{3}=0}^{1}\ldots\sum_{y_{n}=0}^{1} \prod_{k=1}^{n}e^{2\pi{i}x{\frac{y_k}{2^k}}}|y_{1}y_{2}y_{3}\ldots y_{n}\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\bigotimes_{k=1}^{n}(|0\rangle + e^{\frac{2\pi{i}x}{2^k}}|1\rangle) \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\bigotimes_{k=1}^{n}(|0\rangle + e^{2\pi{i}[0.x_{k}\ldots{x_{n}}]}|1\rangle) \end{aligned} \]

The final step in the above expansion is perhaps subtle and worth exploring in more detail. Only the fractional part of \(\frac{x}{2^k}\) will affect the phase of the complex exponential. All powers of 2 greater than or equal to 0 contribute natural multiples of \(2\pi\) which result in 1. \[ e^{\frac{2\pi{i}x}{2^k}} = e^{2\pi{i}\sum_{l=1}^{n}x_{l}2^{n-l-k}} = e^{2\pi{i}[0.x_{k}\ldots{x_{n}}]} \]
